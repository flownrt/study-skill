# Chess – a competency ladder

### For someone going from "knows the moves" to a solid, confident intermediate

Chess is learned by *doing*, so this is not a map of facts to memorize – it's a ladder of things you must be able to **do**, in order, each one verified by play. Every rung pairs a concept with a way to drill it and an observable "you've got it when…" benchmark. The ladder's job is to sequence the practice and tell you what to work on next; the actual learning happens at the board.

The single highest-leverage habit sits in the practice engine below (`P2`, analyzing your own losses). Read that first.

**Sourcing.** Each rung is tagged with where to learn and drill it, all from the established, high-trust chess-instruction canon.

| Tag | Source |
| --- | --- |
| `[LI]` | lichess.org – free lessons, the Puzzles trainer (with spaced practice), Studies, practice positions, and a 7-piece endgame tablebase |

| `[YUS]` | Artur Yusupov, *Build Up Your Chess* / *Boost Your Chess* – a structured, exam-driven curriculum |

| `[CAP]` | José Capablanca, *Chess Fundamentals* – public-domain classic on simple, sound play |
| `[GEN]` | widely-taught fundamentals |

**How the tiers work.** Tier 1 is about not losing – stop handing the game away before strategy even matters. Tier 2 is winning material through tactics, where most games at this level are actually decided. Tier 3 is converting won positions and steering equal ones – endgames and simple planning. The practice engine runs underneath all three, every week.

---

## Tier 1 – Stop losing (board safety)

### B1 · Rules, notation, and piece values `[LI][GEN]`
The complete rules including castling, en passant, and promotion; algebraic notation; and the rough point values (pawn 1, knight/bishop 3, rook 5, queen 9) as a first approximation of a trade's worth. **Drill:** play through lichess's basics lessons and read back a few games in notation. **Got it when:** you can record a game and judge whether a proposed trade wins or loses material without hesitating.

### B2 · Board vision – checks, captures, threats `[HEIS][LI]`
Before deciding anything, scan the position for all **checks, captures, and threats** – both yours and your opponent's. This is the raw perception every later skill depends on; most beginner losses are simply missed checks and captures. **Drill:** on each move, literally list the checks and captures available to both sides before choosing. **Got it when:** you rarely get surprised by a capture or check you "didn't see."

### B3 · The three basic checkmates `[SIL][LI]`
Mating a lone king with king + queen, with king + rook, and with two rooks. These teach you to use the king as an attacker and to drive the enemy king to the edge – the mechanics behind countless endings. **Drill:** practice each against the computer from a random start until automatic. **Got it when:** you deliver K+Q vs K and K+R vs K cleanly, with no stalemate, in well under the 50-move limit, from either colour.

### B4 · The blunder-check routine (keystone skill) `[HEIS][BAR]`
Before every move, ask: *is the move I want to play safe, and what does it let my opponent do?* Check that the piece you're moving isn't needed where it stands and that your move doesn't hang material or walk into a tactic. This one habit gains more rating at the start than any opening study. **Drill:** force a deliberate pause-and-check before each move in slow games. **Got it when:** you play a full long game without hanging a piece for nothing.

### B5 · Opening principles (not memorized lines) `[CAP][BAR]`
Play the opening by principle: fight for the center, develop knights and bishops toward it, castle early for king safety, connect your rooks, don't move the same piece twice without reason, and don't bring the queen out too early. Understanding *why* beats memorizing moves at this level. **Drill:** play your openings by these rules and, afterward, name which principle each early move served. **Got it when:** you reach a sound, developed, castled middlegame from any opening without knowing "theory."

## Tier 2 – Win material (tactics)

This is the engine room. Most games between improving players are decided by who wins material through a tactic – so this tier gets the most repetition (see `P3`).

### T1 · Fork / double attack `[LI][YUS]`
One piece (or pawn) attacks two targets at once; the opponent can only save one. The knight fork is the signature, but any piece can fork. **Drill:** a daily set of fork puzzles. **Got it when:** you solve a mixed set of fork puzzles at roughly 80% and start spotting forks in your own games before they vanish.

### T2 · Pin `[LI][YUS]`
A piece can't (absolute pin, against the king) or shouldn't (relative pin) move because a more valuable piece sits behind it. Pins immobilize a defender so you can pile on it. **Drill:** pin puzzles, plus noticing when *your* pieces are pinned. **Got it when:** you can both exploit a pin (attack the pinned piece again) and avoid leaving your own pieces pinned to the king or queen.

### T3 · Skewer `[LI][GEN]`
A pin in reverse: the valuable piece is in front and, when it moves off the line, the piece behind it falls. Common with rooks and bishops on open lines. **Drill:** skewer puzzles, especially in endgames where they win pawns. **Got it when:** you recognize skewer geometry on open files, ranks, and diagonals on sight.

### T4 · Discovered attack and discovered check `[YUS][LI]`
Moving one piece unveils an attack from the piece behind it; when the unveiled attack is check, the moving piece can grab something with impunity (discovered check is among the most powerful tactics). **Drill:** discovery puzzles. **Got it when:** you can set up and execute a discovered attack, and you check for it before moving a piece that's "shielding" one of your own line-pieces.

### T5 · Removing the defender / overload `[YUS][HEIS]`
Win material by eliminating, deflecting, or overloading the piece that defends a key square or target. If a defender is doing two jobs, attack the second one. **Drill:** "remove the defender" puzzle themes. **Got it when:** before capturing, you reflexively ask "what's defending this, and can I take or distract it first?"

### T6 · Back-rank and basic mating patterns `[LI][YUS]`
The back-rank mate (a king trapped behind its own unmoved pawns) and a handful of recurring mating nets. Recognizing patterns lets you both deliver them and defend against them (the "luft" escape square). **Drill:** mate-in-1 and mate-in-2 puzzles. **Got it when:** you spot back-rank weaknesses in both camps and create luft before it's forced.

### T7 · Counting captures and safe exchanges `[HEIS][GEN]`
On a contested square, count attackers vs. defenders and the order of captures to know whether taking wins, loses, or is even. This is how you avoid "I took and then lost more back." **Drill:** exchange-evaluation puzzles and slow-game practice. **Got it when:** you correctly judge multi-piece exchanges before entering them.

### T8 · Seeing the opponent's threats (defensive tactics) `[HEIS][BAR]`
Every motif above can be used *against* you. After your opponent moves, ask what their move threatens before you continue your own plan. **Drill:** in slow games, state the opponent's threat aloud each move. **Got it when:** you parry one-move tactical threats reliably instead of walking into them.

### T9 · Pattern training as a daily engine `[LI][CC]`
Tactical strength is pattern recognition, and pattern recognition is built by spaced repetition of puzzles – not by reading about tactics. **Drill:** a short daily puzzle set (mixed themes) on lichess or chess.com; revisit themes you fail. **Got it when:** your puzzle rating is trending up and the motifs start appearing to you mid-game without searching.

## Tier 3 – Convert and plan (endgames and strategy)

### E1 · King and pawn vs. king: opposition and the rule of the square `[SIL][LI]`
The foundational endgame: when a lone king can stop a passed pawn (the "rule of the square") and how to use the **opposition** to escort your pawn to promotion or to hold the draw. **Drill:** practice from both sides against the tablebase. **Got it when:** you win or draw K+P vs K correctly from either side, cold.

### E2 · Basic pawn endgames and promotion races `[SIL][CAP]`
Outside passed pawns, the principle of two connected passers, and counting tempi in a promotion race. Pawn endings are pure calculation and underpin every other ending. **Drill:** Silman's pawn-endgame positions; lichess studies. **Got it when:** you can count a king-and-pawn race accurately and know when a passed pawn is "unstoppable."

### E3 · Essential rook endgames: Lucena and Philidor `[SIL][LI]`
Rook endings are the most common endgame, and two positions carry most of the load: the **Lucena** (the winning technique, "building a bridge") and the **Philidor** (the drawing technique). Knowing these two converts or saves a huge fraction of real games. **Drill:** practice both to automaticity against the tablebase. **Got it when:** you execute Lucena and hold Philidor without prompting.

### S1 · Pawn structure `[CAP][YUS]`
Pawns define the terrain: isolated, doubled, and backward pawns are long-term weaknesses; passed pawns are long-term assets; pawn chains point the direction of play. **Drill:** after games, label the structural strengths and weaknesses for both sides. **Got it when:** you can look at a middlegame and name the key structural feature that should guide the plan.

### S2 · Good and bad pieces; outposts `[CAP][YUS]`
Not all pieces are equal: a bishop hemmed in by its own pawns is "bad," a knight on a protected square the enemy can't challenge is an "outpost" and can be worth more than a bishop. Strong play often means improving your worst piece and planting a knight. **Drill:** in analysis, identify each side's best and worst piece. **Got it when:** you routinely find the move that improves your worst-placed piece.

### S3 · Open files, the seventh rank, and rook activity `[CAP][SIL]`
Rooks belong on open and half-open files, and a rook on the seventh rank is a standing threat. Activity usually beats material for rooks. **Drill:** play to seize an open file and double rooks on it; study model games. **Got it when:** you contest and occupy open files deliberately rather than leaving rooks passive.

### S4 · Making a plan when there's no tactic `[CAP][BAR]`
When nothing forcing is available, you need a plan: improve your worst piece, target a weak pawn or square, open a file for your rooks, or gain space – derived from the pawn structure (`S1`). A modest, correct plan beats aimless shuffling. **Drill:** in slow games, before each "quiet" move, state your plan in one sentence. **Got it when:** you can articulate a sensible plan in any quiet middlegame instead of moving randomly.

### S5 · A light, principle-based opening repertoire `[BAR][CC]`
Pick *one* opening for White, one defense against 1.e4, and one against 1.d4 – chosen for clear plans, not sharp memorization. Learn the ideas and typical structures, not twenty moves of theory. **Drill:** play your repertoire repeatedly and review where you left "book." **Got it when:** you reach a comfortable, familiar middlegame from your own openings and know the plan, not just the moves.

## The practice engine (runs under every tier)

These habits are where the rungs above actually get climbed. Without them, the ladder is just reading.

### P1 · Play slow games `[BAR][HEIS]`
Improvement needs thinking time. Play games with a real clock (e.g. 15+10 or longer), not bullet. Fast games drill bad habits – they reward reflexes, not the blunder-check and planning you're trying to build.

### P2 · Analyze your own losses – the highest-leverage habit `[HEIS][BAR]`
After a loss, find the move where it went wrong *yourself* before turning on an engine; then check with the engine and, ideally, a stronger player or community. Your own mistakes are the most relevant curriculum you will ever have. One deeply analyzed loss teaches more than ten new games played and forgotten.

### P3 · Daily tactics `[LI][CC]`
A short, daily puzzle habit (see `T9`) is the most reliable rating-gainer at this level. Consistency beats volume; revisit the themes you miss.

### P4 · Find a community for judgment `[LI][CC]`
The feel for chess that books can't give – when to attack, when a position is "just better" – comes from playing rated games and talking to stronger players. A local club, a coach, or a well-moderated online community turns isolated study into real strength.

## Recommended path

1. **B1 → B2 → B4** – rules, board vision, and the blunder-check. Stop losing before anything else; this alone is most of the early gains.
2. **B3, B5** – the basic mates and opening principles, so you reach playable middlegames and finish won ones.
3. **T1, T2, T3 + T9** – the core tactics plus the daily puzzle habit. Spend the most time here; this tier decides games.
4. **E1, E3** – the two endgames that convert or save the most real games (K+P vs K, and rook endings).
5. **T4–T8, then S1–S5 and E2** – the rest of the tactical toolkit, then structure and planning, as your games start reaching quieter positions where they matter.

Run the **practice engine** (P1–P4) the entire time, from day one.

**You're a solid intermediate when** you (a) rarely hang material in a slow game, (b) reliably win material when your opponent does, (c) convert a basic won endgame without panic, and (d) can state a reasonable plan in a quiet position. Hitting all four means the ladder has done its job – the next ascent is just more of the same, deeper.
