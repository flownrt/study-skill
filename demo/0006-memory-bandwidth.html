The real ceiling is memory bandwidth

Why serving a frontier model is bottlenecked by moving weights, not by doing math — the one idea that explains your bill, your latency, and why context lengths stalled.

Goal: hold your own in technical LLM discussions. After this you can answer "why is output more expensive than input?", "why is there a hard latency floor?", and "why did context lengths stop growing?" — all with the same picture, and read a roofline chart out loud.

Idea Compute is cheap. Bandwidth is the bottleneck.

To generate one token, an accelerator must do two things: move the model's weights out of memory onto the chip, and compute with them. These happen in parallel, so the step takes as long as the slower of the two:

step time = max( bytes moved ÷ memory bandwidth , FLOPs ÷ compute speed )

The surprise — the whole thesis of this debate — is which side usually wins. Modern chips can do hundreds of FLOPs for every byte they can fetch from memory (that ratio, work per byte, is arithmetic intensity). Unless each fetched weight is reused for a lot of math, the chip finishes computing and then sits idle waiting on memory. That regime — memory-bandwidth-bound — is where ordinary LLM serving lives.

The mental modelA serving accelerator is a fast worker starved of parts on a slow conveyor belt. Compute is abundant; bandwidth is scarce. So the whole design game is reusing each fetched byte across as much work as possible — and the chart below is how you see whether you're winning that game.

Why it matters What this one wall actually decides

This isn't a hardware-trivia footnote. The memory-bandwidth wall is the hidden variable behind a surprising share of the pricing, latency, and product decisions you'll argue about.

Your bill: output costs more than input

Processing the prompt (prefill) happens in one big parallel pass — high arithmetic intensity, comfortably compute-bound, cheap per token. Generating the reply (decode) is one token at a time, each step dominated by re-fetching the weights — memory-bound. That asymmetry is why providers charge several times more for output tokens than input tokens. The numbers on the price sheet are the roofline made visible.

Cheap batch APIs — and a latency floor you can't buy past

Because a lone request sits far down the memory-bound ramp, providers pack many users' requests behind a single weight-fetch. That's the biggest single lever on serving cost, and it's why asynchronous batch APIs come at a steep discount: they let the provider fill idle, memory-bound capacity on their own schedule. But the same physics sets a floor nobody can pay under — one conversation's tokens still come out one memory-read at a time. You can raise throughput with batching; you cannot push a single stream's per-token latency below the time it takes to stream the weights past the chip. "Just buy faster GPUs" doesn't help when the extra FLOPs sit idle waiting on the same memory.

Why context lengths plateaued

Long context hits the wall from a second direction. Every token you keep lives in the KV cache, and that cache must be read on every future step — more bytes to move, more memory-bound time per token. That's a big part of why advertised context lengths stalled for a while, and why prompt caching (store a stable prefix's KV so it isn't re-read) and KV-compression schemes exist at all.

Why the models and the chips look the way they do

It even shapes architecture. Mixture-of-Experts routes each token through only a few of its experts, so far fewer weights are fetched per token — a direct assault on the bandwidth bottleneck that pays off at high serving volume. And it's why accelerators are sold on memory bandwidth (terabytes per second of HBM) as loudly as on FLOPs: the high-bandwidth memory is the scarce, expensive part, and newer attention designs (grouped-query, multi-head latent attention) earn their keep by moving fewer bytes.

The levers, and what each does to the wall

Design leverWhat it does to the memory-bandwidth wall
BatchingReuses one weight-fetch across many requests → climbs the ramp toward the compute roof.
MoE / sparsityActivates only a few experts → fewer weight bytes fetched per token.
Prompt cachingReuses a stable prefix's KV cache → skips re-reading it every call.
GQA / MLACompresses the KV cache → fewer bytes to move for long context.
QuantizationFewer bytes per weight (e.g. 8- or 4-bit) → less to move each step.
Faster HBMSteepens the ramp itself → the roof is reached at a smaller batch.
